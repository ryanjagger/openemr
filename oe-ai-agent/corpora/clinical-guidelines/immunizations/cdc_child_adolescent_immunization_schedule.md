# Recommended Child and Adolescent Immunization Schedule

## Overview

The CDC's Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) develops the recommended childhood immunization schedule, updated annually. The schedule applies from birth through 18 years.

**Important context (as of 2026):** Following federal disruption to the ACIP process in 2025, the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) has published an independent schedule, recommended by AAFP, ACOG, IDSA, and other major medical societies. The AAP schedule maintains traditional routine recommendations. For a clinician-facing system, ingest both and flag conflicts.

## Routine Schedule by Age

### Birth
- **Hepatitis B (HepB):** 1st dose

### 1-2 Months
- **Hepatitis B (HepB):** 2nd dose

### 2 Months
- **Rotavirus (RV):** 1st dose
- **DTaP (Diphtheria, Tetanus, Pertussis):** 1st dose
- **Hib (Haemophilus influenzae type b):** 1st dose
- **PCV (Pneumococcal conjugate):** 1st dose
- **IPV (Inactivated polio):** 1st dose

### 4 Months
- **Rotavirus:** 2nd dose
- **DTaP:** 2nd dose
- **Hib:** 2nd dose
- **PCV:** 2nd dose
- **IPV:** 2nd dose

### 6 Months
- **Rotavirus:** 3rd dose (depending on product)
- **DTaP:** 3rd dose
- **Hib:** 3rd dose (depending on product)
- **PCV:** 3rd dose
- **IPV:** 3rd dose
- **Hepatitis B:** 3rd dose (between 6-18 months)
- **Influenza:** Annual, beginning at 6 months
- **COVID-19:** Per current ACIP recommendations
- **RSV (nirsevimab monoclonal antibody):** For infants entering their first RSV season if mother did not receive maternal RSV vaccine

### 12-15 Months
- **MMR (Measles, Mumps, Rubella):** 1st dose
- **Varicella:** 1st dose
- **Hib:** 4th dose (booster)
- **PCV:** 4th dose (booster)
- **Hepatitis A:** 1st dose

### 15-18 Months
- **DTaP:** 4th dose

### 18-23 Months
- **Hepatitis A:** 2nd dose (≥6 months after 1st)

### 4-6 Years
- **DTaP:** 5th dose
- **IPV:** 4th dose
- **MMR:** 2nd dose
- **Varicella:** 2nd dose

### 11-12 Years
- **Tdap:** 1 dose (booster)
- **HPV (Human Papillomavirus):** 2-dose series (can start at age 9)
- **MenACWY (Meningococcal):** 1st dose

### 16 Years
- **MenACWY:** Booster dose
- **MenB (Meningococcal B):** Shared clinical decision-making for healthy adolescents (16-23 years); recommended for those at increased risk

### Annual (All Ages 6 months+)
- **Influenza:** Annual vaccination
- **COVID-19:** Per current ACIP recommendations

## HPV Vaccine Schedule Details

- **Routine age:** 11-12 years (can start at age 9)
- **Catch-up vaccination:** Through age 18 (and into early adulthood for some)
- **2-dose schedule** if first dose given before age 15 (doses 6-12 months apart)
- **3-dose schedule** if first dose given at age 15+ or if immunocompromised (0, 1-2, 6 months)

## Catch-up Schedule

For children behind on vaccinations, catch-up schedules provide minimum intervals between doses. Key principles:
- A vaccine series does not need to be restarted, regardless of the time elapsed
- Doses given ≤4 days before the minimum age or interval are considered valid
- Doses given ≥5 days early should be repeated

## Special Situations

### Pregnancy
- During pregnancy, **Tdap** is recommended at 27-36 weeks of gestation
- **Influenza** vaccine recommended during any trimester
- **RSV vaccine** at 32-36 weeks of gestation during September-January
- **COVID-19** vaccination per current ACIP recommendations
- **Live vaccines (MMR, varicella, LAIV)** are generally contraindicated during pregnancy

### Immunocompromised Children
- Generally avoid live vaccines (MMR, varicella, LAIV, rotavirus, oral typhoid, yellow fever)
- May need additional or higher-potency doses of inactivated vaccines
- Refer to specialized guidance for specific conditions

### Premature Infants
- Vaccinate according to chronological age, not gestational age
- Hepatitis B birth dose may be delayed for very low birth weight infants until 1 month or hospital discharge

### Children with Allergies
- Most vaccines can be administered to children with food or environmental allergies
- Specific contraindications and precautions are listed in product labeling

## Why Vaccinate

Childhood vaccines protect against:
- Diseases that have been nearly eliminated (e.g., polio, diphtheria, measles)
- Diseases that remain present but are largely preventable (e.g., pertussis, varicella, rotavirus)
- Long-term risks (e.g., HPV-related cancers)

Maintaining high vaccination coverage protects individuals, vulnerable populations who cannot be vaccinated, and the broader community through herd immunity.
