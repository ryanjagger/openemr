# Latent Tuberculosis Infection in Adults: Screening

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Asymptomatic adults at increased risk of LTBI | Screen for latent tuberculosis infection. | B |

## Population at Increased Risk

Adults at increased risk for LTBI include:

- **Persons born in or former residents of countries with increased TB prevalence** (most countries in Asia, Africa, the Caribbean, Central and South America, and Eastern Europe)
- **Persons who have lived in high-risk congregate settings**, including:
  - Correctional facilities
  - Long-term care facilities
  - Homeless shelters
- **Healthcare workers** with exposure to active TB cases
- **Other groups at risk** include:
  - Close contacts of persons with active TB
  - Persons with immunocompromising conditions (HIV, transplant recipients, persons taking TNF inhibitors or other immunosuppressants)
  - Persons with substance use disorders

## Screening Tests

Two types of tests are available:

1. **Tuberculin skin test (TST)** — also called Mantoux test or PPD
   - Requires injection and return visit at 48-72 hours
   - Cross-reacts with BCG vaccination

2. **Interferon-gamma release assays (IGRAs)** — blood tests
   - QuantiFERON-TB Gold In-Tube and T-SPOT.TB
   - Single visit
   - Does not cross-react with BCG vaccination
   - Preferred for persons who have received BCG vaccine

Both tests have high specificity (>95%) and acceptable sensitivity. IGRAs are preferred when the patient is unlikely to return for TST reading or has received BCG.

## Confirmatory Testing

A positive screening test should be followed by:
- Symptom assessment for active TB
- Chest radiography to exclude active pulmonary TB

If active TB is excluded, the patient has LTBI and treatment should be considered.

## Treatment

Effective treatment regimens for LTBI:
- **Rifampin (RIF)** — 4 months daily
- **Isoniazid + rifapentine (3HP)** — 12 once-weekly doses (DOT preferred)
- **Isoniazid + rifampin (3HR)** — 3 months daily
- **Isoniazid (INH)** — 6 or 9 months daily

Shorter regimens (3-4 months) generally have higher completion rates than longer regimens (6-9 months).

Treatment of LTBI prevents progression to active TB and reduces transmission.

## Importance

Most cases of active TB in low-prevalence countries like the US occur in persons with reactivation of LTBI acquired earlier. Identifying and treating LTBI is the key strategy for TB elimination in the US.
