# Chlamydia and Gonorrhea: Screening

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sexually active women, including pregnant persons | Screen for chlamydia in all sexually active women 24 years or younger and in women 25 years or older who are at increased risk for infection. | B |
| Sexually active women, including pregnant persons | Screen for gonorrhea in all sexually active women 24 years or younger and in women 25 years or older who are at increased risk for infection. | B |
| Men | Current evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of screening for chlamydia and gonorrhea in men. | I |

## Risk Assessment

**Increased risk** for chlamydia and gonorrhea includes:
- Having a new sex partner
- Having more than one sex partner
- Having a sex partner with concurrent partners
- Having a sex partner with a sexually transmitted infection
- Inconsistent condom use among persons who are not in mutually monogamous relationships
- Previous or coexisting sexually transmitted infection
- Exchanging sex for money or drugs
- History of incarceration

## Screening Tests

Nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs) are recommended for the diagnosis of chlamydia and gonorrhea. NAATs can be performed on:
- Endocervical swabs
- Vaginal swabs (including self-collected)
- Urine
- Pharyngeal swabs (for extragenital screening)
- Rectal swabs (for extragenital screening)

For sexually active women at risk: screen at sites of sexual exposure (cervix, rectum, pharynx as indicated).

## Screening Intervals

Optimal screening intervals are unknown. Annual screening is reasonable for women with risk factors. More frequent screening may be warranted in persons at high risk.

## Treatment

Effective antibiotic regimens are available for both chlamydia and gonorrhea (see CDC STI Treatment Guidelines for current regimens, which evolve as resistance patterns change).

Sex partners should be evaluated, tested, and treated to prevent reinfection and ongoing transmission. Expedited partner therapy (EPT) may be appropriate in some jurisdictions.

## Pregnancy

Screening pregnant women at the first prenatal visit is recommended. Rescreening in the third trimester is recommended for those at continued risk.
