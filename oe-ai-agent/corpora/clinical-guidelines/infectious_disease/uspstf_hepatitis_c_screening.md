# Hepatitis C Virus Infection in Adolescents and Adults: Screening

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Adults aged 18 to 79 years | Screen for hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection in adults aged 18 to 79 years. | B |

## Population

This recommendation applies to all asymptomatic adults aged 18 to 79 years without known liver disease.

Screening should also be considered in younger adolescents (under 18) and adults older than 79 who have risk factors for HCV infection.

## Risk Factors for HCV Infection

Risk factors that may merit screening regardless of age include:
- Past or current injection drug use
- Receipt of blood transfusion or organ transplant before 1992
- Long-term hemodialysis
- Being born to a mother with HCV
- Incarceration
- Intranasal drug use
- Receiving an unregulated tattoo
- Other percutaneous exposures

## Screening Approach

Screen with anti-HCV antibody testing followed by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) testing for HCV RNA if the antibody test is positive. PCR testing for HCV RNA confirms current (active) infection.

## Screening Intervals

Most adults need to be screened only once. Periodic screening should be considered in persons with continued risk for HCV infection (e.g., persons who inject drugs).

## Treatment

Direct-acting antiviral (DAA) regimens are now available that can cure most cases of HCV infection (sustained virologic response, SVR) with 8-12 weeks of oral therapy. Treatment is generally well tolerated. Cure rates exceed 95% in most patient populations.

## Public Health Importance

HCV is the most common chronic blood-borne infection in the US. Approximately 2.4 million persons in the US have chronic HCV infection. Without treatment, chronic HCV can lead to cirrhosis, end-stage liver disease, hepatocellular carcinoma, and death.
