# HIV Infection: Screening

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Adolescents and adults aged 15 to 65 years | Screen for HIV infection. Younger adolescents and older adults who are at increased risk of infection should also be screened. | A |
| Pregnant persons | Screen for HIV infection in all pregnant persons, including those who present in labor or at delivery whose HIV status is unknown. | A |

## Implementation

### Risk Assessment

Persons at increased risk for HIV infection include:
- Men who have sex with men (MSM)
- Sexually active persons living in or seeking care in high-prevalence settings
- Persons with sexual partners who are living with HIV, are bisexual, or inject drugs
- Persons who exchange sex for money or drugs or have sexual partners who do
- Persons who use injection drugs or have sexual partners who do
- Persons who have unprotected vaginal or anal intercourse
- Persons with another sexually transmitted infection (STI)
- Persons who request testing for HIV infection

### Screening Tests

Rapid point-of-care tests can give results in approximately 30 minutes. These can be performed on:
- Oral fluid
- Finger-stick whole blood specimens

The CDC recommends using a fourth-generation antigen/antibody combination immunoassay for initial laboratory testing, followed by an HIV-1/HIV-2 antibody differentiation immunoassay if the initial result is positive.

### Screening Intervals

The optimal interval for rescreening is not known. One reasonable approach is one-time screening of adolescent and adult patients to identify persons who are already HIV-positive, with repeated screening of those who are at increased risk for HIV infection.

For pregnant persons: screen as early as possible during each pregnancy, and rescreening during the third trimester is recommended for those at increased risk.

## Effective Treatment

Patients with newly diagnosed HIV infection should be referred for evaluation and management of HIV. Effective antiretroviral therapy (ART) reduces the risk of disease progression and death, as well as the risk of HIV transmission to sexual partners (treatment as prevention).
