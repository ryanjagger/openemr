# Clostridioides difficile Infection (CDI): Diagnosis, Treatment, and Prevention

## Overview

C. difficile is a leading cause of healthcare-associated infection in the United States, causing approximately 500,000 infections and 15,000-30,000 deaths annually. CDI ranges from mild diarrhea to fulminant colitis with toxic megacolon and death.

## Epidemiology and Risk Factors

**Major risk factors:**
- Recent antibiotic exposure (especially fluoroquinolones, clindamycin, broad-spectrum cephalosporins, carbapenems) — most important modifiable risk factor
- Recent healthcare exposure (hospitalization, long-term care)
- Older age (≥65 years)
- Severe underlying illness
- Immunocompromise
- Prior CDI
- Acid-suppressing medications (proton pump inhibitors, H2 blockers) — modest risk
- Inflammatory bowel disease

## Clinical Presentation

- Watery diarrhea (≥3 loose stools in 24 hours)
- Abdominal pain or cramping
- Fever
- Leukocytosis
- Severe disease may include: ileus, toxic megacolon, perforation, septic shock

## Diagnosis

**Test only patients with clinically significant diarrhea** (≥3 unformed stools in 24 hours) and risk factors. Avoid testing asymptomatic patients (high carriage rate of C. difficile without disease).

**Diagnostic algorithms:**
- **Two-step approach (preferred for many labs):**
  - GDH antigen test (sensitive but not specific) PLUS toxin enzyme immunoassay
  - Reflex to PCR if discordant
- **NAAT (PCR) alone** detects toxigenic C. difficile but cannot distinguish active disease from asymptomatic carriage; may overdiagnose
- **Toxin EIA alone** is specific but less sensitive

Stool specimens should only be tested if liquid (taking the shape of the container).

## Treatment by Severity

### Initial Episode (non-severe)
- **Fidaxomicin** 200 mg PO twice daily × 10 days (preferred — lower recurrence rate)
- OR **Vancomycin** 125 mg PO four times daily × 10 days

### Initial Episode (severe)
*Severe defined as: WBC ≥15,000, serum creatinine >1.5 mg/dL*
- **Fidaxomicin** 200 mg PO twice daily × 10 days
- OR **Vancomycin** 125 mg PO four times daily × 10 days

### Initial Episode (fulminant)
*Fulminant: hypotension/shock, ileus, toxic megacolon*
- **Vancomycin** 500 mg PO/NG four times daily
- **PLUS Metronidazole** 500 mg IV every 8 hours
- **If ileus:** Add vancomycin 500 mg in 100 mL saline as rectal enema every 6 hours
- **Surgical consultation** for severe cases

### First Recurrence
- **Fidaxomicin** 200 mg PO twice daily × 10 days, OR
- **Fidaxomicin** extended-pulsed regimen (200 mg BID × 5 days, then every other day × 20 days), OR
- **Vancomycin** in tapered/pulsed regimen if not used for initial

### Multiple Recurrences
- **Fecal microbiota transplant (FMT)** — recommended after multiple recurrences
- **Bezlotoxumab** (monoclonal antibody) — adjunctive therapy to reduce recurrence in high-risk patients
- **Live biotherapeutic products** (e.g., RBX2660, SER-109) — newer options for recurrence prevention

## Discontinue Inciting Antibiotic

When possible, discontinue or change the antibiotic that precipitated CDI. If continued antibiotics are necessary, narrow-spectrum agents are preferred.

## Infection Prevention

### In acute care:
- **Contact precautions** for all patients with confirmed or suspected CDI
- **Single-patient rooms** (or cohort if necessary)
- **Hand hygiene with soap and water** (NOT alcohol-based hand rub, which does not kill C. difficile spores)
- **Disposable gowns and gloves**
- **Dedicated patient care equipment** when possible
- **Daily cleaning** of patient rooms with sporicidal agent (EPA-registered, e.g., bleach-based)
- **Terminal cleaning** with sporicidal agent on discharge

### Antibiotic stewardship:
- Most important systems-level intervention
- Reduce unnecessary antibiotic use
- Use narrow-spectrum agents when appropriate
- Limit duration of antibiotic therapy

### In long-term care:
- Same principles as acute care
- Continue precautions until 48 hours after diarrhea resolution
- Antibiotic stewardship is critical

### Patient and family education:
- Hand hygiene with soap and water
- Cleaning of bathroom surfaces with bleach-based or other sporicidal cleaner
- Notify clinicians of CDI history at future encounters

## Prevention with Probiotics

Routine probiotic use for primary prevention of CDI is not recommended due to limited evidence. Some patients may benefit, but data are mixed.

## Notable Implementation Notes

- **Test of cure is NOT recommended** for asymptomatic patients after treatment
- **Repeat testing during the same episode** is generally not recommended (low yield, may detect carriage)
- **Travel/community-onset CDI** is increasing; consider in outpatients with diarrhea even without obvious risk factors
