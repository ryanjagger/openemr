# Sexually Transmitted Infections: Behavioral Counseling

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| All sexually active adolescents and adults at increased risk for STIs | Provide behavioral counseling. | B |

## Population

This recommendation applies to:
- All sexually active adolescents (regardless of risk factors)
- Adults at increased risk for STI acquisition

## Increased Risk Factors

Adults at increased risk include those with:
- A current STI or one within the past year
- Multiple sex partners
- A new sex partner
- A sex partner with multiple partners or known STI
- Inconsistent condom use among persons who are not in mutually monogamous relationships
- Exchange of sex for money or drugs
- Belonging to populations with high STI rates (MSM, persons with HIV, persons in correctional facilities, sex workers)
- Membership in sexual networks with higher prevalence (varies by community/region)

## Effective Behavioral Counseling Interventions

The USPSTF identified intensive behavioral interventions (totaling >2 hours of contact) as effective for reducing STI acquisition.

### Common features of effective counseling:
- **Multiple sessions** (typically 2-3 hours total contact time, sometimes more)
- **Individualized risk assessment**
- **Skills training:**
  - Condom use skills
  - Communication and negotiation skills
  - Problem-solving for risky situations
- **Information about STIs:**
  - Modes of transmission
  - Risk factors
  - Recognition and disclosure
  - Treatment importance
- **Goal setting and motivational interviewing**
- **Strategies for behavior change**
- **Cultural and developmental adaptation**

### Settings:
- Primary care
- Sexual health clinics / family planning clinics
- Schools
- Community health settings
- Online platforms

### Delivery formats:
- Group counseling
- Individual counseling
- Mixed format
- Counseling combined with media-based components

## Less Intensive Counseling

Brief in-office counseling alone (e.g., 5-10 minutes during a primary care visit) has not been shown to be as effective. The USPSTF B recommendation specifically refers to more intensive interventions.

In primary care, clinicians can:
- **Identify** sexually active adolescents and adults at increased risk
- **Provide brief counseling** about reducing STI risk
- **Refer to more intensive interventions** when available
- **Address other prevention strategies:**
  - Condom use
  - Limiting number of partners
  - HPV vaccination
  - HIV PrEP
  - Doxy-PEP (for MSM)
  - Hepatitis B vaccination
  - Routine STI screening per USPSTF/CDC

## Importance

STIs are common and have substantial morbidity:
- Approximately 20 million new STIs in the US annually
- Substantial disparities by age, race/ethnicity, geography, sexual orientation
- Untreated STIs can cause:
  - Pelvic inflammatory disease
  - Infertility
  - Ectopic pregnancy
  - Chronic pelvic pain
  - Cervical and other cancers
  - Increased HIV transmission
  - Adverse pregnancy outcomes
  - Neonatal infections (congenital syphilis, ophthalmia neonatorum)

## Related USPSTF Recommendations

- HIV screening
- HIV PrEP
- Chlamydia and gonorrhea screening
- Syphilis screening
- Hepatitis B screening
- Hepatitis C screening
