# Core Elements of Outpatient Antibiotic Stewardship

## Why Antibiotic Stewardship Matters

- At least 30% of antibiotics prescribed in outpatient settings in the US are unnecessary
- Antibiotics cause approximately 1 in 5 emergency department visits for adverse drug events
- Antibiotic resistance is a leading global health threat
- Inappropriate antibiotic use disrupts the microbiome, increases C. difficile risk, and contributes to resistance

## Four Core Elements of Outpatient Antibiotic Stewardship

### 1. Commitment

Demonstrate dedication and accountability for optimizing antibiotic prescribing and patient safety:
- Identify a single leader to direct antibiotic stewardship activities
- Include stewardship-related responsibilities in clinician position descriptions
- Communicate the practice's commitment to patients (e.g., posters in waiting rooms)
- Encourage staff to be ambassadors for stewardship

### 2. Action for Policy and Practice

Implement at least one policy or practice to improve antibiotic prescribing:

**Use evidence-based diagnostic criteria and treatment recommendations:**
- Acute bronchitis: Most cases are viral; antibiotics not recommended
- Pharyngitis: Use Centor criteria + Group A strep testing; antibiotics only for confirmed strep
- Acute uncomplicated cystitis (women): Nitrofurantoin or TMP-SMX or fosfomycin (avoid fluoroquinolones)
- Acute sinusitis: Most cases viral; antibiotics for severe symptoms or symptoms ≥10 days, or worsening after initial improvement
- Acute otitis media: Watchful waiting for many cases; antibiotics if severe or in young infants

**Use delayed prescribing or watchful waiting** when appropriate

**Require explicit written justification** in the medical record for non-recommended antibiotic prescribing

**Provide communication skills training** for clinicians on managing patient expectations

### 3. Tracking and Reporting

Monitor antibiotic prescribing practices and offer regular feedback:
- Self-evaluate and track antibiotic prescribing
- Use peer comparison feedback (e.g., your prescribing rate vs. peers)
- Track for specific conditions (e.g., upper respiratory infections)
- Engage in QI initiatives

### 4. Education and Expertise

Provide educational resources to clinicians and patients:
- Continuing education on appropriate antibiotic use
- Patient education materials (e.g., on viral vs. bacterial infections)
- Ensure access to expert input (e.g., infectious disease consultation)

## Common Conditions and Stewardship Approach

### Upper Respiratory Infections (URIs) — Common Cold
- **Recommended:** No antibiotics
- Symptomatic management only (rest, fluids, OTC symptom relief)
- Educate patients that antibiotics do not help viral infections

### Acute Bronchitis
- **Recommended:** No antibiotics for otherwise healthy adults
- Cough may persist for 2-3 weeks; antibiotics do not shorten duration
- Symptomatic management

### Acute Pharyngitis (Sore Throat)
- Use Centor criteria (fever, tonsillar exudates, tender anterior cervical nodes, absence of cough)
- Test for Group A streptococcus (rapid antigen detection or culture) in adults with high Centor scores
- **Antibiotics only for confirmed Strep A** (penicillin or amoxicillin first-line, 10 days)

### Acute Sinusitis
- Most cases are viral (>90%)
- Antibiotics indicated if:
  - Persistent symptoms ≥10 days without improvement, OR
  - Severe symptoms (high fever, purulent nasal discharge, facial pain) for ≥3-4 days, OR
  - Worsening symptoms after initial improvement (double-worsening)
- First-line: amoxicillin-clavulanate (5-7 days)

### Acute Otitis Media
- Watchful waiting (without antibiotics, with follow-up if symptoms persist) for:
  - Children ≥2 years with non-severe unilateral AOM
  - Children ≥6 months with non-severe AOM
- Antibiotics for: bilateral AOM in young children, severe symptoms, otorrhea
- First-line: amoxicillin (high-dose: 80-90 mg/kg/day in 2 divided doses)

### Acute Uncomplicated Cystitis (Women)
- **First-line options:**
  - Nitrofurantoin 100 mg PO BID × 5 days
  - TMP-SMX 160/800 mg PO BID × 3 days (if local resistance <20%)
  - Fosfomycin 3 g PO single dose
- **Avoid as first-line:** Fluoroquinolones (reserve for complicated infections), beta-lactams (less effective)
- Pyelonephritis: outpatient fluoroquinolone or cephalosporin; admit if severely ill or unable to tolerate oral

### Skin and Soft Tissue Infections
- Mild non-purulent cellulitis: cephalexin or dicloxacillin (cover Streptococcus)
- Purulent SSTI: I&D primary; antibiotics if systemic symptoms or large/severe (cover MRSA: TMP-SMX, doxycycline, clindamycin)

### Acute Diarrhea (Adults)
- Most cases are self-limited viral or non-infectious
- Antibiotics indicated for: severe traveler's diarrhea, suspected bacterial dysentery (bloody stool, high fever), confirmed C. difficile
- Avoid antibiotics for suspected Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (may increase HUS risk)

## Patient Communication Strategies

When patients expect antibiotics for non-bacterial illness:
- Acknowledge their concerns and discomfort
- Explain why antibiotics will not help
- Provide a clear plan for symptom management
- Set expectations for symptom duration
- Offer "safety-netting" instructions: when to return for re-evaluation
- Consider a delayed prescription if appropriate (with instructions to fill only if symptoms worsen)

## Special Populations

- **Children:** Generally same principles; AAP and IDSA guidelines for specific conditions
- **Older adults:** Particular caution; antibiotic adverse effects more common; C. diff risk higher
- **Immunocompromised:** Lower threshold for treatment; consult specialists when uncertain
