# Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm: Screening

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Men aged 65 to 75 years who have ever smoked | One-time screening for abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) with ultrasonography. | B |
| Men aged 65 to 75 years who have never smoked | Selectively offer screening for AAA with ultrasonography rather than routinely screening all men in this group. | C |
| Women aged 65 to 75 years who have ever smoked or have a family history of AAA | Current evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of screening for AAA. | I |
| Women who have never smoked and have no family history of AAA | Recommend AGAINST screening for AAA. | D |

## Definition

Abdominal aortic aneurysm is generally defined as an abdominal aortic diameter of 3.0 cm or larger. Most AAAs are asymptomatic until rupture, which has high mortality.

## Screening Test

**Ultrasonography** is highly sensitive (94% to 100%) and specific (98% to 100%) for detecting AAA, has minimal harms, can be performed quickly, and does not require radiation exposure or contrast.

Screening is **one-time** for those eligible. The USPSTF found no evidence regarding the optimal frequency of repeat screening or the benefits of repeat screening for adults with normal aortic diameter on initial screening.

## Surveillance and Treatment

If AAA is detected:
- **3.0 to 3.9 cm:** Periodic surveillance with ultrasonography
- **4.0 to 5.4 cm:** More frequent surveillance
- **5.5 cm or larger** (or rapidly expanding or symptomatic): Surgical repair generally indicated

Surgical repair options:
- Open repair (traditional surgical approach)
- Endovascular aneurysm repair (EVAR) — less invasive, faster recovery

## Risk Factors

Major risk factors for AAA:
- **Age** (rare under 60, increases sharply with age)
- **Male sex** (4-6 times higher than women)
- **Smoking** (strongest modifiable risk factor)
- **Family history of AAA** (first-degree relative)
- **History of cardiovascular or peripheral vascular disease**
- **Atherosclerosis**
- **Hypertension**
- **Hyperlipidemia**

## Population Notes

The recommendation against screening women who have never smoked and have no family history of AAA reflects the very low prevalence in this group, where harms of screening (including overdiagnosis and unnecessary procedures) likely outweigh benefits.
