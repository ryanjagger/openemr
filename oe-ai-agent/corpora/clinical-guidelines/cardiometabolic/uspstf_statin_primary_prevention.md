# Statin Use for the Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease in Adults

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Adults aged 40 to 75 years with 1+ CVD risk factor and estimated 10-year CVD risk ≥10% | Prescribe a statin for primary prevention of CVD. | B |
| Adults aged 40 to 75 years with 1+ CVD risk factor and estimated 10-year CVD risk 7.5% to <10% | Selectively offer a statin. The likelihood of benefit is smaller in this group than in persons with 10-year risk ≥10%. | C |
| Adults 76 years or older | Current evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms. | I |

CVD risk factors include: dyslipidemia, diabetes, hypertension, or smoking.

## Population

These recommendations apply to adults 40 years or older without a history of known CVD. They do **not** apply to adults with:
- LDL-C >190 mg/dL (4.92 mmol/L)
- Known familial hypercholesterolemia

(These populations are at very high risk for CVD; refer to other guidelines for management.)

## Implementation

For adults aged 40 to 75 years:
1. Determine whether the patient has a cardiovascular risk factor (dyslipidemia, diabetes, hypertension, or smoking).
2. Estimate CVD risk using a CVD risk estimator (ACC/AHA Pooled Cohort Equations is the only US-based prediction tool with external validation).
3. In patients with a CVD risk factor and an estimated 10-year CVD risk ≥10%, initiate a moderate-intensity statin after discussion of rationale and patient agreement.
4. In patients with a CVD risk factor and 10-year CVD risk 7.5% to <10%, selectively offer a statin, taking patient values and preferences into account.

For adults 76 years or older: insufficient evidence to recommend for or against starting a statin.

## Statin Intensity

Use of moderate-intensity statin therapy seems reasonable for the primary prevention of CVD in most persons. Limited data directly compare the effects of different statin intensities on health outcomes. Most clinical trials reviewed by the USPSTF used moderate-intensity statin therapy.

## Risk Assessment

The ACC/AHA Pooled Cohort Equations may be used to estimate 10-year risk of CVD. The estimator includes:
- Age
- Sex
- Race (Black vs non-Black)
- Cholesterol levels
- Systolic blood pressure level
- Antihypertensive treatment
- Presence of diabetes
- Smoking status

Outcomes: hard clinical outcomes (myocardial infarction and death from coronary heart disease; ischemic stroke and stroke-related death).

**Caveats:**
- Age is one of the strongest risk factors for CVD; risk estimates are heavily influenced by age.
- Concerns about calibration exist, with external validation studies showing overprediction in broad populations and underprediction in disadvantaged communities.
- Predictions are estimates and should not be treated as precise.

## Importance

Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of mortality in the US, accounting for more than 1 in 4 deaths. In 2019, an estimated 558,000 deaths were caused by coronary heart disease and 109,000 deaths were caused by ischemic stroke.

## Equity Considerations

Multiple studies have reported inequities in statin use:
- Black adults have a decreased likelihood of being prescribed a statin compared with White adults.
- Among persons eligible for statin use, statin use was higher among non-Hispanic White (58.3%) compared with non-Hispanic Asian (49.2%), non-Hispanic Black (44.3%), or Hispanic (33.7%) persons.
- Having no health insurance was associated with decreased statin use.
- It is essential to equitably improve statin use across races, ethnicities, and socioeconomic levels.

## Other Considerations

Among both sexes, Black persons have the highest prevalence of CVD. On average, men experience CVD events earlier in life compared with women.
