# Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy: Screening

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Asymptomatic pregnant persons | Screen for hypertensive disorders in pregnant persons with blood pressure measurements throughout pregnancy. | B |

## Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

The hypertensive disorders of pregnancy include:

1. **Chronic hypertension** — present before pregnancy or before 20 weeks gestation
2. **Gestational hypertension** — new-onset hypertension at ≥20 weeks gestation without proteinuria or other features of preeclampsia
3. **Preeclampsia** — new-onset hypertension at ≥20 weeks gestation with proteinuria OR other end-organ dysfunction (thrombocytopenia, renal insufficiency, impaired liver function, pulmonary edema, neurologic symptoms)
4. **Eclampsia** — preeclampsia plus new-onset seizures
5. **Chronic hypertension with superimposed preeclampsia**

## Diagnostic Thresholds

**Hypertension in pregnancy:**
- Systolic BP ≥140 mm Hg, OR
- Diastolic BP ≥90 mm Hg
- Two measurements at least 4 hours apart

**Severe hypertension in pregnancy:**
- Systolic BP ≥160 mm Hg, OR
- Diastolic BP ≥110 mm Hg

**Preeclampsia diagnostic criteria** (hypertension PLUS one of):
- Proteinuria (≥300 mg/24 hours, protein/creatinine ratio ≥0.3, or dipstick ≥1+)
- Thrombocytopenia (platelets <100,000/μL)
- Impaired liver function (transaminases >2x upper normal)
- Renal insufficiency (creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling)
- Pulmonary edema
- New-onset headache or visual disturbances

## Screening Approach

Screen with blood pressure measurements at each prenatal visit throughout pregnancy.

**Standard prenatal visit schedule:**
- Every 4 weeks until 28 weeks
- Every 2 weeks from 28-36 weeks
- Weekly from 36 weeks until delivery

(More frequent visits may be needed for high-risk pregnancies.)

## Blood Pressure Measurement

Use standardized technique:
- Patient seated for 5 minutes
- Arm at heart level, supported
- Appropriate cuff size (cuff bladder length 80% and width 40% of arm circumference)
- Two readings, 1-2 minutes apart, averaged
- Both arms at first visit, then use higher arm

## Risk Factors for Preeclampsia

**High-risk factors** (presence of any one suggests aspirin prophylaxis):
- Prior preeclampsia
- Multifetal gestation
- Chronic hypertension
- Type 1 or type 2 diabetes
- Renal disease
- Autoimmune disease (SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome)

**Moderate-risk factors** (combination suggests aspirin prophylaxis):
- Nulliparity
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Family history of preeclampsia
- Black race (reflects socioeconomic disparities)
- Low socioeconomic status
- Age ≥35
- History of low birth weight or SGA infant
- Adverse pregnancy outcome
- Interpregnancy interval >10 years

## Management of Confirmed Hypertensive Disorders

### Outpatient management (mild-moderate gestational hypertension):
- Increased frequency of prenatal visits
- Maternal symptom monitoring
- Fetal monitoring
- Consider antihypertensive medication if BP persistently ≥140/90
- Plan delivery at 37 weeks gestation (or earlier if complications)

### Antihypertensives in pregnancy:
- **First-line:** labetalol, nifedipine, methyldopa
- **Avoid:** ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers (teratogenic)
- **Severe hypertension** (≥160/110): IV labetalol or hydralazine; oral nifedipine

### Severe preeclampsia/eclampsia:
- Delivery is definitive treatment
- Magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis (eclampsia prevention)
- Antihypertensives for severe BP elevations
- Close maternal and fetal monitoring

## Postpartum Follow-up

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy continue to confer risk in the postpartum period:
- BP monitoring within 7-10 days postpartum (sooner if severe)
- Continue or initiate antihypertensives as needed
- Counsel on long-term cardiovascular risk
- Persons with HDP have 2-fold increased risk of cardiovascular disease later in life

## Importance

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy are a leading cause of maternal morbidity and mortality:
- Affect 5-10% of pregnancies in the US
- Account for approximately 15% of pregnancy-related deaths
- Lead causes of maternal stroke, organ damage
- Associated with preterm birth, intrauterine growth restriction, perinatal mortality
- Significant racial disparities exist; Black women are 3-4 times more likely to die from HDP-related causes
