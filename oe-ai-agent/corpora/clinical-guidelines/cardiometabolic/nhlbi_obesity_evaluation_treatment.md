# Identification, Evaluation, and Treatment of Overweight and Obesity in Adults

## Overview

The NHLBI Clinical Guidelines on the Identification, Evaluation, and Treatment of Overweight and Obesity in Adults (originally published 1998, with 2013 evidence review) provide a foundational framework for the assessment and management of overweight and obesity in primary care. While more recent guidelines (e.g., 2013 AHA/ACC/TOS, ADA Standards of Care) have updated specific treatment recommendations, the assessment framework and evidence base remain widely cited.

## Definitions and Classification

### BMI Categories (kg/m²)
| Classification | BMI |
|---|---|
| Underweight | <18.5 |
| Normal | 18.5-24.9 |
| Overweight | 25.0-29.9 |
| Obesity, Class 1 | 30.0-34.9 |
| Obesity, Class 2 | 35.0-39.9 |
| Obesity, Class 3 (severe) | ≥40 |

### Waist Circumference Risk Thresholds

Increased risk of obesity-related comorbidities is associated with:
- **Men:** waist circumference >102 cm (>40 inches)
- **Women:** waist circumference >88 cm (>35 inches)

Lower thresholds may be appropriate for some populations (e.g., Asian populations may have higher metabolic risk at lower BMI and waist circumference).

## Initial Assessment

For all adult patients, assess:
1. **BMI** (calculate from height and weight)
2. **Waist circumference** (especially for those with BMI 25-34.9)
3. **Risk factors and comorbidities:**
   - Hypertension
   - Type 2 diabetes
   - Dyslipidemia
   - Coronary artery disease
   - Other atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease
   - Sleep apnea
   - Osteoarthritis
   - Cholelithiasis
   - Stress incontinence
   - Cancer (multiple types associated with obesity)

## Risk Stratification

A patient is classified as having very high absolute risk for obesity-related morbidity if they have any of:
- Established coronary heart disease
- Other atherosclerotic disease
- Type 2 diabetes
- Sleep apnea

Patients are classified as having high absolute risk if they have ≥3 of:
- Hypertension (BP ≥140/90 or on antihypertensives)
- Cigarette smoking
- High LDL cholesterol (≥160 mg/dL)
- Low HDL cholesterol (<35 mg/dL)
- Impaired fasting glucose (110-125 mg/dL)
- Family history of premature CHD (in first-degree male relative <55 years; female <65 years)
- Age ≥45 years (men) or ≥55 years (women)

## Treatment Approach

The framework is a **stepped approach** based on BMI, waist circumference, and risk factors:

### BMI 25-26.9 with no comorbidities
- Weight maintenance (prevent further weight gain)
- Healthy eating and physical activity
- Routine follow-up

### BMI 27-29.9 with comorbidities OR BMI ≥30
- **Lifestyle intervention:** Diet, physical activity, behavior therapy (foundation of all treatment)
- Goal: 5-10% weight loss over 6 months

### BMI ≥30 with comorbidities OR BMI ≥30 if lifestyle insufficient
- **Add pharmacotherapy** (consider current FDA-approved options including GLP-1 receptor agonists, naltrexone-bupropion, orlistat, phentermine-topiramate)

### BMI ≥40 OR BMI ≥35 with serious comorbidities
- **Consider bariatric surgery** in qualified centers
- Should be combined with lifestyle and behavioral support

## Lifestyle Components (Foundation of All Treatment)

### Dietary Therapy
- **Goal:** Calorie deficit of 500-1000 kcal/day for weight loss of 1-2 lb/week
- **Macronutrient composition:** Various dietary patterns are effective; the best diet is one the patient can adhere to long-term
- **Examples of evidence-based dietary patterns:**
  - Mediterranean diet
  - DASH diet
  - Low-carbohydrate diets
  - Low-fat diets
  - Plant-based diets

### Physical Activity
- **Initial:** 30 minutes of moderate-intensity activity most days
- **Progressive:** 200-300 minutes per week for weight maintenance
- **Plus:** Resistance training 2-3 times per week
- **Important:** Diet alone is more effective than exercise alone for weight loss; both combined are most effective

### Behavior Therapy
Key components:
- Self-monitoring of weight, food intake, physical activity
- Stimulus control (modify environment to support healthy choices)
- Goal setting (specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound)
- Problem solving for barriers
- Cognitive restructuring (challenging negative self-talk)
- Stress management
- Social support
- Relapse prevention

## Realistic Weight Loss Goals

- **5-10% weight loss** is associated with meaningful improvements in:
  - Blood pressure
  - Glycemic control / diabetes prevention
  - Lipid profile
  - Cardiovascular outcomes
  - Sleep apnea
  - Joint pain
- This realistic goal is important for patient motivation and clinical decision-making

## Maintenance

After initial weight loss:
- Continue lifestyle modifications indefinitely
- Regular follow-up (monthly or more frequently)
- Weight regain is common; ongoing support and intervention needed

## Special Considerations

### Older Adults
- Consider risks of weight loss (sarcopenia, osteoporosis, frailty)
- Combine modest weight loss with resistance exercise

### Pregnancy
- Weight loss is generally not recommended during pregnancy
- Limit gestational weight gain per IOM guidelines

### Children and Adolescents
- Different framework; refer to AAP guidance
- USPSTF recommends comprehensive intensive behavioral intervention for children ≥6 years with high BMI

## Updated Guidance (Beyond NHLBI 1998)

Newer authoritative sources include:
- **2013 AHA/ACC/TOS Guideline for the Management of Overweight and Obesity in Adults** (more recent specific treatment recommendations)
- **2022 American Gastroenterological Association** guideline on pharmacologic treatment
- **2023 American Academy of Pediatrics** guideline for children and adolescents
- **ADA Standards of Care, Section 8** (annual updates on obesity in diabetes)

Many of these reference the foundational NHLBI framework summarized here.
