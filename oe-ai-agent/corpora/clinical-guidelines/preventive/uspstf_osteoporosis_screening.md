# Osteoporosis to Prevent Fractures: Screening

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Women 65 years or older | Screen for osteoporosis to prevent osteoporotic fractures. | B |
| Postmenopausal women younger than 65 years at increased risk for an osteoporotic fracture (as estimated by clinical risk assessment) | Screen for osteoporosis to prevent osteoporotic fractures. | B |
| Men | Current evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of screening for osteoporosis. | I |

## Risk Assessment

For postmenopausal women under 65, clinical risk assessment tools can identify those at higher risk who may benefit from screening:

- **FRAX (Fracture Risk Assessment Tool)** — the most widely studied tool; calculates 10-year risk of major osteoporotic fracture and hip fracture
- **OST (Osteoporosis Self-Assessment Tool)** — uses age and weight
- **ORAI (Osteoporosis Risk Assessment Instrument)** — uses age, weight, and current estrogen use
- **OSIRIS (Osteoporosis Index of Risk)** — uses age, weight, prior fracture, and current estrogen use

**Common risk factors for osteoporosis and fracture:**
- Older age
- Low body weight
- Family history of osteoporosis or hip fracture
- Personal history of fracture (especially after age 50)
- Smoking
- Excessive alcohol intake
- Glucocorticoid use
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Other secondary causes (hyperthyroidism, hyperparathyroidism, diabetes, etc.)
- Estrogen deficiency (early menopause, surgical menopause, hypogonadism)
- Low calcium and vitamin D intake
- Sedentary lifestyle

## Screening Tests

**Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA)** of the hip and lumbar spine is the most commonly used and most studied screening test for osteoporosis. It measures bone mineral density and provides a T-score:

- **Normal:** T-score ≥ -1.0
- **Osteopenia (low bone mass):** T-score between -1.0 and -2.5
- **Osteoporosis:** T-score ≤ -2.5

Quantitative ultrasonography of the calcaneus is an alternative to DXA but does not provide T-scores comparable to DXA.

## Screening Intervals

Optimal intervals are uncertain. The USPSTF found no evidence on the benefits of repeating screening over time. Some experts suggest reasonable intervals based on baseline T-score.

## Treatment

Effective treatments to reduce fracture risk in women with osteoporosis include:
- Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate, zoledronic acid)
- Denosumab (RANK ligand inhibitor)
- Selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs) such as raloxifene
- Anabolic agents (teriparatide, abaloparatide, romosozumab) for high-risk patients

Adequate calcium and vitamin D intake, weight-bearing exercise, fall prevention, smoking cessation, and limiting alcohol are recommended for all patients.

## Population

This recommendation applies to:
- All women 65 years or older
- Postmenopausal women younger than 65 years who are at increased risk for an osteoporotic fracture as estimated by clinical risk assessment

This recommendation does NOT apply to:
- Persons with secondary causes of osteoporosis
- Persons with prior history of low-trauma fractures (who should be evaluated and treated rather than just screened)
