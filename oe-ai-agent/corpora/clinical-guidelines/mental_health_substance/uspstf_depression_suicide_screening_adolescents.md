# Depression and Suicide Risk in Children and Adolescents: Screening

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Adolescents aged 12 to 18 years | Screen for major depressive disorder (MDD). | B |
| Children 11 years or younger | Current evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of screening for major depressive disorder. | I |
| Children and adolescents (all ages) | Current evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of screening for suicide risk. | I |

## Population

This recommendation applies to adolescents aged 12 to 18 years who do not have a diagnosed mental health condition and are not showing recognized signs or symptoms of depression or suicide risk.

## Screening Tools for Depression

Validated screening tools include:

- **Patient Health Questionnaire (PHQ-9)** — adolescent version (PHQ-A)
- **Beck Depression Inventory-II** (for adolescents)
- **Center for Epidemiologic Studies Depression Scale for Children (CES-DC)**
- **Mood and Feelings Questionnaire (MFQ)**

Most tools take 5-10 minutes to administer.

## Definition of Major Depressive Disorder in Adolescents

Per DSM-5, MDD is defined by at least 2 weeks of at least 5 of the following symptoms (with at least one being depressed/irritable mood or anhedonia):

- Depressed or irritable mood
- Diminished interest or pleasure
- Significant weight loss/gain or appetite changes
- Insomnia or hypersomnia
- Psychomotor agitation or retardation
- Fatigue or loss of energy
- Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
- Diminished concentration or indecisiveness
- Recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation

Note: Irritable mood (rather than just depressed mood) qualifies for adolescents.

## Effective Treatments

For diagnosed MDD in adolescents:

### Psychotherapy:
- **Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)** — most evidence-based
- **Interpersonal therapy for adolescents (IPT-A)**
- Often delivered in individual format

### Pharmacotherapy:
- **Fluoxetine** — only FDA-approved SSRI for pediatric depression (ages 8+)
- **Escitalopram** — FDA-approved for ages 12+
- Other SSRIs sometimes used off-label

### Combined treatment:
- CBT + SSRI is generally more effective than either alone for moderate-severe depression
- Treatment for Adolescents with Depression Study (TADS) supports combined treatment

### Important considerations:
- All antidepressants carry FDA black-box warning about increased suicidal ideation in children/adolescents (highest risk in first weeks of treatment)
- Close monitoring during initiation and dose changes (weekly for first 4 weeks, biweekly for next 4, then monthly)
- Family involvement in treatment is important
- Address comorbid conditions (anxiety, substance use)

## Suicide Risk

While the USPSTF concluded that current evidence is insufficient for routine suicide risk screening, clinicians should:
- **Always assess suicide risk** in adolescents who screen positive for depression
- **Always assess suicide risk** in adolescents presenting with mental health concerns or behavioral changes
- **Maintain clinical vigilance** for warning signs (statements about wanting to die, hopelessness, withdrawal, giving away possessions, recent loss)

**Suicide risk assessment tools include:**
- Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS)
- Ask Suicide-Screening Questions (ASQ)

- **Crisis Text Line:** text HOME to 741741
- **The Trevor Project (LGBTQ youth):** 1-866-488-7386 or text START to 678-678

## Importance

- Approximately 13% of adolescents experience a major depressive episode in a given year
- Suicide is the second leading cause of death for adolescents 10-14 and 15-24 in the US
- Rates of depression and suicide in adolescents have been increasing
- Early identification and treatment can substantially reduce morbidity and mortality
