# BRCA-Related Cancer: Risk Assessment, Genetic Counseling, and Genetic Testing

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Women with a personal or family history of breast, ovarian, tubal, or peritoneal cancer, OR who have an ancestry associated with BRCA1/2 gene mutations | Assess with an appropriate brief familial risk assessment tool. Women with a positive result should receive genetic counseling and, if indicated after counseling, genetic testing. | B |
| Women whose personal or family history or ancestry is not associated with potentially harmful BRCA1/2 gene mutations | Recommend AGAINST routine risk assessment, genetic counseling, or genetic testing. | D |

## Risk Assessment Tools

Brief familial risk assessment tools help primary care clinicians identify women who may benefit from genetic counseling. Examples include:

- **Ontario Family History Assessment Tool**
- **Manchester Scoring System**
- **Referral Screening Tool**
- **Pedigree Assessment Tool**
- **7-Question Family History Screening Tool (FHS-7)**
- **International Breast Cancer Intervention Study (IBIS) instrument**
- **Breast Cancer Genetics Referral Screening Tool (B-RST)**

## Population at Increased Risk

Women who should be evaluated with a familial risk assessment tool include those with:
- Personal history of breast cancer (especially before age 50, triple-negative, bilateral, or in a male relative)
- Personal history of ovarian, fallopian tube, or peritoneal cancer
- Family history of breast cancer in close relatives, especially:
  - Multiple affected relatives
  - Relatives with bilateral breast cancer
  - Relatives diagnosed before age 50
  - Male relatives with breast cancer
- Family history of ovarian, fallopian tube, or peritoneal cancer
- Family history of pancreatic cancer or aggressive prostate cancer (especially with associated breast cancer)
- Ancestry associated with increased BRCA1/2 mutation prevalence (e.g., Ashkenazi Jewish, Norwegian, Dutch, Icelandic)
- A known BRCA1/2 mutation in the family

## Genetic Counseling and Testing

For women with a positive risk assessment result:

1. **Genetic counseling** by trained healthcare professionals (genetic counselors, geneticists, or appropriately trained primary care or oncology clinicians) should occur BEFORE testing
2. **Genetic counseling components include:**
   - Discussion of personal and family cancer risks
   - Implications of testing for the patient and relatives
   - Possible test results (positive, negative, variant of uncertain significance)
   - Limitations of testing
   - Psychological implications
   - Cost and insurance considerations
   - Privacy considerations
3. **Genetic testing** if indicated after counseling

## Risk Management for BRCA Mutation Carriers

Women found to have a BRCA1/2 mutation may benefit from:
- **Enhanced screening:** Earlier and more frequent breast imaging (annual MRI plus mammography starting at age 25-30)
- **Risk-reducing medication:** Tamoxifen or aromatase inhibitors
- **Risk-reducing surgery:**
  - Bilateral mastectomy
  - Bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (typically recommended at age 35-40 after childbearing for BRCA1; 40-45 for BRCA2)

## Importance

BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations confer:
- Lifetime breast cancer risk of approximately 45-65% (vs. 12% in general population)
- Lifetime ovarian cancer risk of approximately 15-40% (vs. 1-2% in general population)
- Increased risk of fallopian tube, peritoneal, and male breast cancers, and possibly pancreatic and prostate cancers

Mutations are present in approximately 1 in 400 women in the general US population, but in 1 in 40 in Ashkenazi Jewish populations.

## Related USPSTF Recommendations

- Breast cancer screening
- Medication use to reduce risk of breast cancer
