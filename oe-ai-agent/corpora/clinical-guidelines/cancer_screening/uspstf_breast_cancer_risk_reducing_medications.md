# Breast Cancer: Medication Use to Reduce Risk

## Recommendation Summary

| Population | Recommendation | Grade |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Women aged 35 years or older who are at increased risk for breast cancer and at low risk for adverse medication effects | Offer to prescribe risk-reducing medications, such as tamoxifen, raloxifene, or aromatase inhibitors. | B |
| Women who are not at increased risk for breast cancer | Recommend AGAINST routine use of risk-reducing medications. | D |

## Population

Women 35 years or older without a previous diagnosis of breast cancer (including ductal carcinoma in situ or lobular carcinoma in situ).

## Risk Assessment

A formal breast cancer risk assessment tool should be used. Examples include:
- **National Cancer Institute Breast Cancer Risk Assessment Tool (Gail Model)**
- **Tyrer-Cuzick (IBIS) tool**
- **BRCAPRO**
- **Claus Model**
- **BCSC** (Breast Cancer Surveillance Consortium tool)

A 5-year risk of breast cancer of ≥3% is generally considered "increased risk." Some clinicians use a higher threshold (e.g., 1.66% 5-year risk by Gail Model, the FDA threshold for tamoxifen).

### Increased risk includes:
- Strong family history of breast cancer
- BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutation carriers
- History of LCIS or atypical ductal/lobular hyperplasia
- History of chest radiation between ages 10-30
- Older age (with other risk factors)

## Risk-Reducing Medications

### Selective Estrogen Receptor Modulators (SERMs):
- **Tamoxifen** — approved for premenopausal and postmenopausal women
  - Typical regimen: 20 mg daily for 5 years
- **Raloxifene** — approved for postmenopausal women only
  - Typical regimen: 60 mg daily for 5 years

### Aromatase Inhibitors (off-label for breast cancer prevention; postmenopausal only):
- **Anastrozole**
- **Exemestane**

## Effectiveness

Risk-reducing medications reduce the incidence of invasive estrogen-receptor positive breast cancer:
- Tamoxifen reduces incidence by approximately 30-50%
- Raloxifene is somewhat less effective than tamoxifen
- Aromatase inhibitors reduce incidence by approximately 50-65% in postmenopausal women

## Potential Harms

**Tamoxifen:**
- Increased risk of venous thromboembolism (deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism)
- Increased risk of endometrial cancer (in women with intact uterus)
- Cataracts
- Vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes)
- Sexual dysfunction

**Raloxifene:**
- Increased risk of venous thromboembolism (less than tamoxifen)
- Vasomotor symptoms
- Lower endometrial cancer risk compared to tamoxifen

**Aromatase inhibitors:**
- Bone loss/osteoporosis
- Joint pain/musculoskeletal symptoms
- Vasomotor symptoms
- Vaginal dryness

## Considerations Against Use

Generally avoid risk-reducing medications in women with:
- History of thromboembolic disease
- Pregnancy or planning pregnancy
- Severe hot flashes
- Active liver disease
- Untreated osteoporosis (for aromatase inhibitors)

## Related USPSTF Recommendations

- Breast cancer screening
- BRCA-related cancer risk assessment, genetic counseling, and genetic testing
